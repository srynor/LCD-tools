Sales Cold Call Guide

From "Hello?" to the First PO

How to open a cold call with a data center prospect who has never heard of LAN Cables Direct, position every product we sell, survive the pushback, and walk away with a next step on the calendar — every time.

Know This Cold

The Value Proposition

Before you dial, you need one sentence you can say in your sleep. The prospect has never heard of us, so the value prop has to stand on their pain, not our brand.

The one-liner: "We're the direct supplier/manufacturer of the physical layer of the data center — fiber trunks, optics, DAC, copper, and power — tested before it ships, at your dock in 5–8 business days, without a distributor markup in between."

Speed

95% of custom fiber and copper assemblies dispatch in 5–8 business days. Standard optics ship same-day from stock. The industry norm for custom MTP trunks is 4–6 weeks. This gap is our sharpest weapon — cabling should never be the thing holding up a turn-up.

Quality You Can Name

Corning glass. US Conec ferrules. SENKO connectors. Every assembly insertion-loss and return-loss tested; every transceiver coded, burned in, and verified on target silicon. No refurbished material, ever. When a prospect doubts an unknown brand, named components are the answer.

Direct Price

No channel markup, no rebate structure, no distributor in the middle. Same parts the channel houses sell — without their overhead. On third-party optics versus OEM list, the savings are not 10–15%; they are often a multiple.

Accountability

One person, one phone number, one accountability chain. 15-year warranty on passive fiber and copper, 5-year on active optics and AOCs — against the OEM's typical 90 days to 1 year. Warranties are how a new vendor borrows trust it hasn't earned yet.

Positioning rule for brand-new prospects: never open with who we are — open with the two pains every infrastructure team recognizes instantly: lead times that slip builds and OEM optic pricing. Our name comes after they recognize the problem. A brand they've never heard of is a reason to hang up; a problem they live with every week is a reason to listen. Which pain you lead with depends on who answers — match it in The Four People Who Pick Up.
Before the Playbook: the List

Who to Call — and Who to Skip

The best opener in the world is wasted on the wrong company. Qualify the list before you qualify the prospect — an hour of list discipline is worth a week of great calls to dead accounts.

Reviewed & approved by sales leadership

The bullseye — call these first

  • Colocation / multi-tenant operators mid-expansion — recurring cross-connect and cage-build spend, and lead-time pain hits them hardest. Our cage-to-meet-me-room story fits exactly.
  • AI / GPU cluster builders — 400G/800G optic volume, dense MTP backbone, brutally compressed timelines. The optics line item alone justifies the call.
  • Enterprises running their own data centers — refresh cycles (10G→25G→100G) and a resented OEM optics bill. Slower to move, but sticky once landed.
  • Regional cloud / hosting providers — hyperscaler problems without hyperscaler purchasing leverage. Direct pricing means more to them than to anyone.

Common thread: they own their network gear, order cabling or optics at least quarterly, and have a build, refresh, or expansion inside 12 months.

Worth a call — with eyes open

  • Integrators and electrical contractors doing DC builds — real volume, but price-driven and loyal to their distributors. We sell both through them and around them — quote the contractor's BOM and pursue the end customer directly; the two don't conflict.
  • MSPs managing client data centers — one relationship, many BOMs, but they may mark us up to their clients — accepted; their reorder volume is ours either way.

Skip, politely

  • All-in public cloud companies — there is no layer 1 for them to buy.
  • Colo tenants who don't own equipment — their operator buys the cabling. Ask who that is, exit warm, call the operator.
  • One-off small cabling jobs (an office, a closet) — point them at the website and configurator; don't spend dials here.

Who to ask for

First: Director/VP of Infrastructure, Data Center Operations Manager, Network Engineering lead — people who feel lead-time pain personally. Second: procurement — but only after a technical champion exists; procurement-first ends as a line card in a drawer. C-level only at small operators where the CTO still signs POs. How the conversation itself changes with the title: see The Four People Who Pick Up.

The three disqualifying questions

  1. "Do you run your own network gear, or is it all through your colo or MSP?" — No gear means no sale. Ask who handles it, exit warm, and call them instead.
  2. "How often does cabling or optics get ordered — monthly, quarterly, or when something breaks?" — Break-fix-only with no builds coming belongs on the nurture list, not the dial list.
  3. "Any build, refresh, or expansion on the calendar in the next 12 months?" — None, plus a low run-rate: park for 90 days with a trigger alert. Don't keep dialing hope.

Walk-away rules

  • A hard no is a no. Log it, leave the door open, stop calling.
  • Asked to be removed from the list: remove, log, honor it — without exception.
  • Single-vendor mandate enforced by procurement, with no technical contact willing to champion — there is no play here. Log it and move on.
  • Annual cable/optic spend below $25K — the math doesn't support pursuit; configurator link and a quarterly email instead.
Same Pitch, Wrong Ear = Dead Call

The Four People Who Pick Up

You qualified the company before dialing; you qualify the person in the first ten seconds. The opener (Step 1) never changes — honesty and permission work on everyone. The hook is what flexes: each of these four lives with a different pain, and leading with the wrong one ends the call. Listen for the job in how they answer, then run their script.

Runs the Building The Data Center Operator / Ops Manager
Who they areColo and facilities side — uptime, tenant turn-ups, cross-connects, cage builds, the loading dock. In a colo, tenants often bring their own network gear, so this person may buy lots of fiber and patch and no optics at all.
The pain that landsA sold cage that isn't lit. Revenue starts when the tenant turns up, and cabling stuck in a distributor's queue is dead money on the floor.
"I'm with a data center supplier/manufacturer — we build the trunk fiber, cross-connect assemblies, and patch that cage builds wait on. The reason I'm calling: most colo teams tell me tenant turn-ups wait on cabling more than on anything else. When a cage is sold, how long until it's lit?"
Discovery questions"What's your turnaround promise on a cross-connect — and which part of it do you actually control?" • "When a cage build slips a week, who hears about it first — you or the tenant?"
Don't lead withOEM optic pricing — they may not buy a single transceiver. Network-engineering depth. Lead with turn-up speed and the 5–8 day promise.
A win looks likeA quote on the next cage build or cross-connect stock order — trunks, patch, and power cords on one PO.
Owns the Schedule The Data Center Engineer (Design / Build)
Who they areThe person with the drawings — specs the build, owns the project schedule, usually doesn't sign the PO. What they spec, purchasing buys.
The pain that landsThe schedule. Long-lead cabling forces them to design around a six-week hole, and an install-day surprise (wrong polarity, a failed link) wrecks the week.
"I'm with a data center supplier/manufacturer — we supply the pre-terminated fiber that build schedules hinge on. A win looks likeGetting written into the next BOM quote — and the specialty-cable offer ("send the drawing, quote in 24 hours") is the fastest way to their heart. Solve one weird problem and you're their vendor.
Gets Paged at 2 A.M. The Network Engineer
Who they areRuns the switches and the links. Rarely signs a PO — but in practice holds a veto over every optic that enters the building. No transceiver survives this person's distrust.
The pain that landsRisk, not price. Their nightmare is being the one blamed when a third-party optic flaps a link — and a "cheap optic" pitch makes that nightmare more vivid, not less.

Don't lead withSavings percentages. Price enters later, framed as their win: "you walk the quote into your boss's office and look like the hero." Never ask them to buy — they can't.
A win looks likeA technical champion: quote their highest-volume optic, lab pilot after they confirm the pricing looks good, and permission to use their name when you call the budget owner.
Does the Work The Hands-On Tech / NOC
Who they areRacking, patching, tracing, the 2 a.m. fixes. Owns nothing on paper and everything in practice — and very often the person who answers the number you dialed.
The pain that landsProduct ergonomics: snapped boots, stiff jackets, mystery polarity, gray-on-gray tracing at 2 a.m. They can't buy from you — but they know exactly what's bad about what's being bought.
"Sounds like I've got the person who actually does the work, not the person who signs for it — so I won't pitch you. One question and I'm gone: who decides what cabling and optics get bought there? … Appreciated. And between us — anything they keep buying that you hate working with?"
Why that last question is goldThe tech's complaint becomes your discovery for the real buyer: "your floor team mentioned the patch boots keep snapping…" is the warmest cold open there is.
Don'tPitch pricing, ask them to pass along a brochure (it dies in a drawer), or treat them as an obstacle. They're the best scout in the building — and sometimes next year's ops manager.
A win looks likeThe right name, the best calling window, and one product gripe from the floor to open the real buyer's call with.
The fifth person — procurement: per the rules above, procurement comes after a technical champion exists, never first. When you do reach them, their pain isn't speed or specs — it's risk and paperwork: lead with second-source resilience ("one supplier for trunks is a single point of failure"), simple vendor onboarding, and warranty terms. A procurement-first call without a champion is a line card in a drawer.

The Anatomy of the Call

The Cold Call, Step by Step

The goal of a cold call is not a sale. It is the next small step: a 15-minute meeting, or a one-page price comparison with a scheduled follow-up. Every line below is built around that. Click each stage.

0 Before You Dial — 60 Seconds of Prep

Find one trigger and one name. A trigger is anything that means they're buying: an expansion announcement, a new hall, job postings for DC techs, a colo win, an AI cluster press release. The name is the infrastructure / network / procurement lead. One minute on their website and LinkedIn is enough — this is a cold call, not a thesis.

Why: one specific, true detail ("saw you're standing up the Ashburn hall") makes you sound like the 1-in-50 caller who did homework. Specificity is credibility you don't have to claim.

Decide your wedge product before dialing: optics if they run their own network gear, trunks if they're mid-build, DACs if you know nothing. Have one real number ready (e.g., OEM list vs. our price on 100G SR4). And decide who you expect to answer — the pain you lead with changes with the job (see The Four People Who Pick Up).

1 The Opener — First 10 Seconds (No Company Name Yet)

They answer. Your job in the first ten seconds is to not sound like every other sales call, and to get permission to keep talking. You do not lead with your name or the company name — you lead with honesty and a category.

"Hi, is this Dana? … Dana, I'll be straight with you — this is a cold call. You can hang up on me now, or give me 27 seconds and then decide. Fair enough?"
Why it works: a pattern interrupt — admitting it's a cold call is the one thing telemarketers never do, so the brain's hang-up reflex misfires. Offering the hang-up restores their sense of autonomy: people resist hardest when they feel trapped, and relax when given an exit. "27 seconds" is oddly specific, which reads as honest and usually earns a smile.
Alternative (qualifier opener): "Hi Dana — quick question before anything else: are you the right person for the cabling and optics side of your data center builds?"
Why: opens with a question they can answer easily, gets a first small "yes," and confirms you're talking to the right person. Slightly more salesy-sounding than the honest opener — use it when you're unsure of your target.
What you never do: "Hi! How are you today?!" (instant telemarketer pattern-match), or opening with "This is [name] from LAN Cables Direct" — an unknown brand name is the easiest thing in the world to say no to. The brand comes later, when it has context. But note the companion rule in Step 2: if they ask who you are, you answer immediately.
2 The Hook — Category + Reason + Pain (Seconds 10–40)

They gave you the 27 seconds. Spend it on a three-part sentence: what category we are, why you're calling, and a pain hypothesis ending in a question.

"I'm with a data center supplier/manufacturer — we supply the fiber trunks, optics, and DAC that builds end up waiting on. The reason I'm calling: most infrastructure teams I talk to are quoted four-to-six weeks on custom MTP and pay OEM list on optics. Is either of those true over there?"
Why it works: "data center supplier/manufacturer" gives them a category to file you under without a brand to dismiss. "The reason I'm calling" exploits the because effect — people comply far more with requests that carry a stated reason, almost regardless of the reason. The pain hypothesis ends in a question, which hands the conversation to them — and "most infrastructure teams I talk to" is quiet social proof that people like them take this call.
Match the pain to the person: the script above is tuned for an infrastructure director. A colo ops manager hears tenant turn-ups; a build engineer hears schedule; a network engineer hears 2 a.m. blame, never price. Word-for-word variants for each are in The Four People Who Pick Up.
The "who are you?" rule: at some point they'll ask "who is this exactly?" Answer immediately and fully: "LAN Cables Direct — I'm Mike. We're a direct supplier/manufacturer, no distributor in between." Delaying the brand until there's context is technique. Dodging a direct question is evasion, and evasion ends calls. Never dodge twice.
3 Discovery — Ask, Don't Pitch (Minutes 1–3)

If they engage at all, resist the urge to pitch. Ask, label, and quantify. The prospect should do most of the talking. Your best questions:

  • "On your last trunk order — how long from PO to dock?"
  • "When the cabling slips, what happens to the turn-up date?"
  • "Rough math — what does a day of idle hall cost you?"
  • "What are you paying per 100G SR4 today — OEM or third-party?"
  • "Who feels it first when parts run short mid-build — you or your contractor?"
Why: people aren't persuaded by your claims; they're persuaded by their own answers. The cost-of-delay question converts a shrug ("lead times are what they are") into a number — and a number creates urgency you don't have to manufacture. Use labeling ("sounds like waiting has become part of the plan…") to show you heard them before asking the next question.
4 The Micro-Commitment Ladder — Small Steps to the Big Prize

Never ask a stranger for the account. Ask for the smallest next step they'd be unreasonable to refuse, then the next. The ladder:

  1. 27 seconds of attention →
  2. One answer to a discovery question →
  3. One price-and-lead-time comparison on their highest-volume SKU (one part, one page) →
  4. 15 minutes with the budget owner →
  5. A pilot: five optics in the lab, or DACs/patch on the next order (samples and pilot units only after they've seen the quote and confirmed the pricing looks good) →
  6. A line on the next BOM → the account.
"Here's a low-effort way to find out if I'm wasting your time: pick the part you buy most — optics, trunks, DAC — and I'll send you one price-and-lead-time comparison against what you're paying. One part, one page. If it's not compelling, you've lost five minutes. Worth a look?"
Why it works: commitment and consistency — people who take a small step act consistently with it and become far more likely to take the next one. "Maybe I'm wasting your time" is a takeaway that lowers the pressure, and "one part, one page" makes the ask feel almost free.
5 The Close — Always Leave With a Calendar Entry
"I'll send the SR4 comparison today. If the math is interesting, the useful next step is 15 minutes with whoever owns the optics budget — does Thursday morning or Friday afternoon work better for a placeholder?"
Why: the alternative choice close — "Thursday or Friday" converts a yes/no decision into a scheduling decision. "Placeholder" lowers the stakes of accepting. Confirm their email address while still on the phone and send the invite before you hang up — an invite accepted live is ten times stickier than one sent later.

End at the peak. Once you have the next step, stop selling and get off the phone warmly: "That's all I needed — talk Thursday." The last ten seconds are what they remember.

6 Gatekeepers & Voicemail

Gatekeepers are allies, not obstacles. Ask for help — people like to help — and give them an honest one-liner they can repeat:

"Morning — hoping you can point me in the right direction. Who looks after purchasing for the cabling and optics on your data center builds? … And is now a decent time to catch them?"
Why: confidence and brevity get transfers; evasion gets "send it to info@". Never lie to a gatekeeper ("it's a personal call") — it gets discovered and burns the whole account.

Voicemail exists to warm the next call, not to get a callback. Keep it under 30 seconds, leave one concrete number, and pair it with an email the same hour:

"Dana, this is Mike with LAN Cables Direct — we're a data center supplier/manufacturer, fiber and optics. One number for you: most teams pay $X for a 100G SR4 on the OEM line; ours is $Y, tested, five-year warranty. I'm emailing you the comparison right now — same subject line as this call. I'll try you again Thursday morning."
Why: the voicemail-plus-email pair builds familiarity (mere exposure — people warm to what they've encountered before), so Thursday's call is no longer fully cold. Note: in a voicemail you do lead with name and company — the no-brand-first rule is for live dialogue, where you can build context before the label.
7 After the Call — Same Day, Every Time

Within the hour: a three-line recap email with the one comparison you promised, the brochure attached, and the agreed next step restated with the date. Subject line referencing the call ("Per our call — the SR4 numbers"). Log the wedge product, the objections you heard, and the trigger for next time.

Why: speed of follow-up is the cheapest, most reliable differentiator in sales — and it quietly proves the entire value proposition: we're the fast ones.
When They Push Back

Objection Handling

An objection on a cold call is a reflex, not a decision. Rule one: never argue — agree, reframe, and ask a question. Read the objection, think about what you'd say, then reveal the play.

"We already have a supplier."
"I'd be worried if you didn't. I'm not calling to replace anyone — most of our accounts started us as the backup quote. Honest question: when your current supplier quotes four weeks on trunks, what's your plan B?"
Why it works: agreeing disarms the reflex; "backup quote" asks for nothing, so there's nothing to refuse. The plan-B question plants loss aversion — suddenly having no backup feels like the risk.
"Not interested."
"Fair enough — I'll let you go. One question before I do, then I'm gone: when a build is waiting on cabling stuck in a distributor's queue, what does that cost you per day?"
Why it works: the takeaway. "I'll let you go" removes the pressure they were bracing against; with nothing to push back on, the single question often gets answered — and an answer reopens the call. If they still say no: thank them warmly and exit. A graceful exit keeps the number callable in six months.
"Just send me an email."
"Happy to — and so I don't send you the generic brochure, one question: what do you buy most — optics, MTP trunks, or DAC? … I'll build the email around that and call you Thursday to see if the math is worth a conversation. Fair?"
Why it works: "send me an email" is usually a polite hang-up. Agreeing costs nothing; the qualifying question plus a named follow-up day converts the brush-off into two micro-commitments. An unqualified brochure email joins the other forty in the graveyard.
"We only buy OEM optics — our vendor won't support third-party."
"That's the most common thing I hear, so let me be precise. The optic in the OEM box is built to the same MSA standard as ours; ours is coded for your switch and burned in on the same silicon. Vendors can't void support for third-party optics just for being third-party — and ours carry five years against their ninety days. Don't take my word for it: let me quote your highest-volume optic, and if the number looks good, I'll put five in your lab — on us — and you can see if TAC even notices."
Why it works: meets the fear head-on with facts, then drops the ask from "switch your optics" to "look at one quote" — with the free lab pilot as the prize behind it. The warranty contrast reframes who's actually taking the risk. Sample policy: the pilot units ship only after they've seen the quote and confirmed the pricing looks good. Rep glossary: MSA is the multi-source industry standard every transceiver maker builds to; TAC is the switch vendor's technical-support team — it's the engineer's own vocabulary, so use it.
"We buy everything through our VAR / distributor."
"And I'm not asking you to fire them — VARs are great at a lot of things. But on cables and optics you're paying their margin for a part they just pass through. Do one thing: take a line item off your last invoice — whichever one — and let me quote it. If the gap's not worth a conversation, I'll never mention it again."
Why it works: never attack the incumbent relationship — shrink the comparison to one line item, where the direct-price story is undeniable and the effort to check is near zero.
"I've never heard of you. How do I know your stuff isn't junk?"
"Fair — you haven't, and skepticism is the right default. So judge the parts, not the pitch: Corning glass, US Conec ferrules, SENKO connectors — the same names in the assemblies you already buy. Every assembly ships with its insertion-loss test report in the bag, and we back passive product for fifteen years. Tell me the part you buy most — I'll quote it, and if the pricing looks right to you, I'll send a sample your techs can put on a meter."
Why it works: validating the skepticism builds trust; named components let us borrow credibility from brands they already trust; the test report and 15-year warranty shift risk from them to us. The quote-then-sample sequence puts the verdict in their hands — people trust their own conclusions — while honoring the sample policy: price first, sample after they confirm it looks good.
"I'm too busy — call me some other time."
"Totally get it — I'll be brief now and gone in ten seconds. I'm with a data center supplier/manufacturer; the call is about cutting your cabling lead times roughly in half. Does Thursday at 9 or Friday at 2 work better for ten minutes?"
Why it works: respect the time pressure but don't accept "some other time" — vague deferrals never convert. One sentence of value plus an alternative-choice close turns "later" into a calendar slot. If they pick one, send the invite immediately.
"Who is this? What company are you with?"
"LAN Cables Direct — I'm Mike. We're the direct supplier/manufacturer of data center cabling and optics, no distributor in between. And the reason for the call is the part that matters to you: …"
Why it works: the no-brand-first technique has a hard limit — the moment they ask, you answer, fully and without hesitation, then bridge straight back to their pain. Delaying the brand once builds curiosity; dodging a direct question reads as evasive, and evasion ends calls. Never dodge twice.
"Your price sounds too good — what's the catch?"
"The catch is what's missing: there's no distributor between us, so there's no channel markup and no rebate structure baked into the price. Same components, same testing — we just sell it to you instead of selling it to someone who sells it to you."
Why it works: "too cheap" is a quality objection in disguise. Giving a structural reason for the price (the because effect again) lets them accept the number without concluding the product is inferior.
"We're not buying anything right now."
"Perfect timing, honestly — nobody makes a good vendor decision mid-crisis. When's the next build or refresh on the calendar? … Then here's all I want: I'll send one price sheet now so you have a benchmark, and I'll check in a month before that date. Fair?"
Why it works: reframes "no budget now" as the ideal moment to evaluate calmly. The benchmark price sheet plants an anchor they'll compare every future quote against, and the dated follow-up is a micro-commitment that keeps the door legitimately open.
"I'm not the right person — purchasing handles that."
"Honestly, I'd rather talk to you first anyway — you're the one who feels it when the parts are late or flaky; purchasing just signs. Two quick things and I'm gone: what do you buy most on the cabling side? … And when I call purchasing, can I say you and I spoke?"
Why it works: this is the #1 thing engineers and ops folks say, and it's an opportunity, not a brush-off. A call to purchasing that opens with a colleague's name is no longer cold — internal referral beats any script. Always ask permission to use the name; an ambush name-drop burns both contacts. And having helped you once, they're inclined to keep helping — that's commitment-consistency turning a wrong number into a champion.
"You've reached the NOC — we don't take sales calls."
"And I won't give you one. You'd know better than anyone though — who owns cabling and optics purchasing there? … Appreciated. Last one, just between us: anything they keep buying that you hate working with?"
Why it works: NOC techs screen pitches all day, so refusing to pitch is the pattern interrupt. The routing question respects their time; the gripe question turns the screen into a scout — a complaint from the floor ("the patch boots keep snapping") is the warmest possible opener for the real buyer's call. Never pitch the tech, never ask them to pass along a brochure — both die in the NOC.
One Call Is Not the Game

The Cadence: 8 Touches in 21 Days

Most cold calls end in a no-answer, and most meetings come from touch three or later. The call flow above is what you run whenever they pick up — this is what you run when they don't. One sequence per prospect, then park it.

Reviewed & approved by sales leadership

Values marked [$ — pricing sheet] are filled in by the rep from the current pricing sheet before dialing or sending — they change too often to hardcode here.

The sequence

  1. Day 1, morning — Call #1. They pick up: run the playbook. No answer: voicemail #1 (the Step 6 script) plus the pairing email within the hour.
  2. Day 3 — Call #2. Different time of day. No voicemail — two messages in three days reads as pestering.
  3. Day 5 — Email: "One number." Template below. Shortest email of the sequence, and often the one that gets the reply.
  4. Day 8 — Call #3 + voicemail #2. Script below — it references the Day 5 email, so the two touches reinforce each other.
  5. Day 11 — LinkedIn connect. Short note, zero pitch: "Called a couple times about cabling lead times — figured I'd put a face to the voicemails." Within policy — keep it to the connect note; the selling stays on the phone and in email.
  6. Day 15 — Email: the standing offer. One line: reply with your highest-volume part number and the quote is back in 24 hours — and if the pricing looks good to you, a sample follows so your techs can judge the build quality and the lead time firsthand. Sample policy: no samples until a quote has been delivered and the prospect confirms the pricing looks good. Price earns the interest; the sample closes it.
  7. Day 18 — Call #4. No voicemail.
  8. Day 21 — The breakup email. Template below. Then park for 90 days — recycle only on a fresh trigger (expansion news, DC-tech job postings, a funding announcement). "Just checking in" without news is noise.

Voicemail #2 (Day 8)

"Dana, Mike with LAN Cables Direct again — I emailed you Tuesday, subject line 'One number.' Short version: [$ — pricing sheet] for a 100G SR4 against OEM list. If my math is wrong for your setup, tell me and I'll leave you alone. Otherwise I'll try you Thursday morning."
Why: referencing the email by subject line makes both touches feel like one coherent thread instead of scattered spam — and "tell me and I'll leave you alone" is a takeaway that invites a reply either way.

Email templates

Subject: Per my voicemail — the SR4 numbers

Dana — Mike with LAN Cables Direct, the data center supplier/manufacturer from your voicemail just now. One attachment: 100G SR4, our price next to OEM list [$ — pricing sheet]. Tested, coded for your switches, five-year warranty. If the math is interesting, I'm calling Thursday morning — or grab 15 minutes here: [rep's calendar link].
Subject: One number

OEM list on a 100G SR4: [$ — pricing sheet]. Ours: [$ — pricing sheet] — tested, coded, five-year warranty against their ninety days. Multiply by your port count. Worth 15 minutes?
Subject: Closing the file

Dana — four calls and four emails says the timing's off, so I'll stop here. Two things before I go: the benchmark pricing I sent doesn't expire — use it against your next quote even if it's never with us. And when a lead time finally bites or an optics bill stings, my direct line is below. I'll check back if I see news of the next build.
Why the breakup works: it routinely gets the highest reply rate of the whole sequence — the takeaway ("I'll stop here") removes the pressure, and losing access to a benchmark feels like a loss even to someone who never replied. Loss aversion does the closing.

Rules of the cadence

  • Three voicemails maximum per sequence. Calls without voicemails still count as touches.
  • Rotate call windows — 8–9am, the lunch edge, 4:30–5:30pm. Gatekeepers thin out at the edges of the day; directors answer their own phones early.
  • Any reply or pickup exits the cadence and enters the call flow. The sequence is for silence, not conversations.
  • Opt-out or hard no: stop immediately, log it, honor it — including do-not-call requests, without exception.
  • One sequence per prospect per quarter. If touch 8 lands nothing, the answer is a better trigger next quarter — not touch 9.
Why These Scripts Work

The Psychology Cheat Sheet

Every line in this playbook uses one of these. Know the principle and you can improvise the words.

Pattern Interrupt

Brains auto-reject anything that sounds like a telemarketer. Say the thing they never say ("this is a cold call") and the hang-up reflex misfires, buying you ten seconds of genuine attention.

Autonomy & Permission

People resist hardest when they feel trapped. "You can hang up on me" hands control back — and people who feel free to refuse are dramatically more likely to listen.

The "Because" Effect

Requests with a stated reason get far more compliance, almost regardless of the reason. Always give "the reason I'm calling" within the first two sentences.

Labeling

Name their emotion or situation out loud: "sounds like waiting has become part of the plan." Feeling understood lowers defenses faster than any argument.

Foot-in-the-Door

Small commitments lead to big ones, because people act consistently with what they've already agreed to. 27 seconds → one answer → one page → 15 minutes → pilot → account.

Social Proof

"Most infrastructure teams I talk to…" signals that people like them take this call and have this problem. Strangers trust the behavior of their peers more than your claims.

Loss Aversion

Losses loom about twice as large as gains. "What does a day of idle hall cost you?" and "what's your plan B when they quote four weeks?" sell harder than any discount.

The Takeaway

Lowering pressure raises interest: "Maybe I'm wasting your time," "I'll let you go — one question first." When there's nothing to push against, people stop pushing.

Anchoring

The first number in the conversation becomes the reference point. Quote OEM list price first, then ours — and send a benchmark price sheet even to prospects who "aren't buying."

Alternative Choice Close

"Thursday morning or Friday afternoon?" replaces a yes/no decision with a scheduling decision. Either answer is a yes.

Mere Exposure

People warm to what they've encountered before. Voicemail + same-hour email + named follow-up day means call two is never truly cold.

Peak–End Rule

People remember the peak moment and the ending. Close on the win, restate the next step, get off the phone warmly — never keep selling past the yes.

The Cold Call Simulator

A branching practice call. Pick what you'd actually say; the prospect reacts, and the coach tells you why each move works — or doesn't. Try to book the meeting. Then try to get hung up on, just to see how easy it is.
