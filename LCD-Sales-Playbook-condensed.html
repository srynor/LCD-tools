The Big Picture - What Is All This Stuff?

▾
The Simple Version

Think about roads and cars.

Your house connects to the grocery store by roads. Cars carry people and groceries on those roads.

A data center works the same way. Computers need to talk to each other. They send information (like cars) through cables (like roads). The faster the road, the more cars can travel at once.

We build the roads.

LAN Cables Direct sells the cables, connectors, and optical parts that connect computers together inside big buildings called data centers. These buildings power everything - your Netflix, your email, your bank's website, AI chatbots, all of it.

Data travels through cables the same way cars travel on roads. Wider roads (faster cables) carry more traffic.

Two Types of "Roads"

There are two fundamental ways to send data through a cable:

💡
Fiber Optic
Uses pulses of light through thin glass strands. Extremely fast, long distances.
⚡
Copper
Uses electrical signals through copper wires. Shorter distances, lower cost per port.
Technical Detail

Why this matters for sales: Your customer's choice between fiber and copper depends on distance, speed requirements, and budget. Most modern data centers use fiber for the backbone (long runs between switches) and copper for short connections within a rack or between adjacent racks. Knowing which to recommend and when is the core of your product knowledge.

Inside a Data Center

▾
The Simple Version

A data center is a big building full of tall metal shelves called racks. Each rack holds computers (called servers) and networking equipment (called switches).

Think of a big library. The racks are bookshelves. The servers are books. The cables are the hallways connecting everything together.

Every cable in the building has to be neat, organized, and able to be changed quickly when something breaks or gets upgraded. That's called structured cabling - having a plan for where every cable goes.

Two racks in a data center. Each rack has a switch at top, a patch panel for cable management, servers below, and power at the bottom. The fiber trunk connects the two racks.

Key Components in a Rack

  • Switch (Top of Rack / ToR): The traffic controller. Every device in the rack connects to it. It decides where data goes next.
  • Patch Panel: An organized wall of ports. Cables from the permanent building infrastructure land here. Short jumper cables connect from the patch panel to the switch. This way, you never unplug permanent cables - you only swap the short ones.
  • Servers: The computers doing the actual work - storing data, running applications, processing AI models.
  • PDU (Power Distribution Unit): A smart power strip. Distributes electricity to every device in the rack. Our IEC power cords connect servers and switches to the PDU.
Architecture Note

Leaf-Spine vs. Traditional: Modern data centers use a "leaf-spine" architecture. Every "leaf" switch (in each rack) connects to every "spine" switch (at the top of the network). This creates multiple paths for data, eliminating bottlenecks. The cables connecting leaf to spine switches are where our MTP fiber trunks and transceivers live. High-volume, high-margin territory.

Fiber Optic Cables - Our Core Product

▾
The Simple Version

Imagine you have a flashlight and a very long, very thin glass tube. You shine the flashlight into one end, and the light comes out the other end - even if the tube is curved.

That's fiber optic cable. Instead of a flashlight, a tiny laser sends flashes of light through a strand of glass thinner than a human hair. Each flash is a piece of data. The light bounces along the inside of the glass until it reaches the other end.

Why light instead of electricity? Light travels farther without losing strength, carries more data, and doesn't get confused by nearby electrical signals.

Fiber optic cable sends pulses of laser light through a glass core. Multimode uses a wider core for short distances. Singlemode uses a narrow core for long distances.

OM4 is our bread and butter. Most data center fiber runs are OM4 multimode. It handles 10G, 25G, 40G, and 100G at the distances data centers need (under 150 meters between racks). OS2 singlemode is for longer campus or building-to-building runs.

Our Fiber Cable Products

▶ MTP Trunk Cables (Backbone)
Simple

Think of a thick bundle of garden hoses all taped together. Each hose carries water. An MTP trunk cable is a thick bundle of fiber strands (12, 24, 48, 72, 96, or even 144 fibers) all in one cable, with a special connector on each end called an MTP connector.

MTP stands for "Multi-fiber Termination Push-on." It's like plugging in a USB cable, but it connects 12 or 24 fibers at once instead of one at a time.

Why MTP trunks matter: Installing fiber one strand at a time would take forever. MTP trunks let you connect 12 or 24 fibers in one click. They come pre-terminated from the factory (already built with connectors), so there's no field splicing or polishing. Plug in and go.

What we sell:

  • MTP-12 Trunks - 12 fibers per connector. Standard for 40G/100G.
  • MTP-24 Trunks - 24 fibers per connector. Growing for 100G/400G.
  • Base-8 Trunks - 8-fiber configurations. Optimized for 25G/100G deployments using 2-fiber per lane optics.
  • Available in OM3, OM4, OM5, and OS2.
  • Custom lengths. Business days, not weeks.
Technical Detail

Polarity matters. MTP connectors come in Type A (key up to key up, straight), Type B (key up to key down, reversed), and Type C (key up to key up, pairs flipped). The customer's infrastructure design dictates which polarity they need. Always confirm polarity before quoting. Wrong polarity = light doesn't reach the receiver = the link is dead.

Pin vs. No-Pin (Male vs. Female): MTP connectors have alignment pins on one end (male) that slot into the other end (female). Trunks are typically female-to-female. The cassette or adapter panel has the male (pinned) side. Always confirm.

Sales Angle

Our MTP trunks use US CONEC MTP connectors and Corning glass. These are the industry-standard components. Some competitors use generic ferrules that cause higher insertion loss and inconsistent polarity. Our cables are factory-tested and ship with test reports.

▶ MTP-to-LC Breakout / Harness / Array Cables
Simple

Remember the garden hose bundle? Now imagine one end stays bundled (MTP connector), but the other end splits apart into individual hoses (LC connectors). That's a breakout cable. It takes one big multi-fiber connector and fans it out into individual fiber connections.

Why breakouts exist: The MTP trunk brings a fat bundle of fibers to the rack. But switches and servers have individual ports. The breakout cable (also called a harness or array cable) converts from MTP to individual LC duplex connectors that plug directly into transceivers.

Common configurations: MTP-12 to 6x LC duplex, MTP-24 to 12x LC duplex, MTP-8 to 4x LC duplex.

▶ LC Duplex Patch Cables
Simple

These are the short jumper cables. LC connectors on both ends. They connect a switch port to a patch panel port, or a patch panel to a server. "Duplex" means two fibers side by side - one sends, one receives.

Available in all fiber types (OM3, OM4, OS2). Typical lengths: 1m, 2m, 3m, 5m. LC is the dominant connector in modern data centers - small form factor, high density.

Copper Cables - CAT6A & CAT6

▾
The Simple Version

If fiber cables use light, copper cables use electricity. They look like the cable that connects your home internet router to your computer - a plastic plug on each end (called RJ45) with a cable in between.

Inside the cable, there are 8 thin copper wires twisted together in pairs (4 pairs). The twisting helps prevent signals from getting scrambled by nearby electronics.

A CAT6A cable has 4 twisted pairs of copper wire inside a shielded jacket, terminated with RJ45 connectors.

🔔
Always recommend CAT6A for data centers. The extra shielding and full 100-meter 10G support is what enterprise customers need. CAT6 is fine for office runs, but data center specs call for CAT6A.
Sales Context

Copper is typically used for within-rack or same-row connections (server to ToR switch). Runs over 5 meters in a data center usually switch to fiber or DAC. Our copper patch cables come in standard lengths from 0.5m to 15m. Custom lengths available.

When a customer asks for "Ethernet cables" or "network cables," they mean copper. Clarify the category (CAT6A vs CAT6) and the length needed.

Optical Transceivers - The Translators

▾
The Simple Version

A switch speaks "electricity." A fiber cable speaks "light." They don't understand each other.

A transceiver is the translator. It plugs into the switch and converts electrical signals to light (and back again). It's a small, hot-swappable module about the size of a pack of gum.

"Hot-swappable" means you can plug it in or pull it out while the switch is running. No need to shut anything down.

Transceivers plug into ports on the switch. They convert between the switch's electrical signals and the cable's optical (light) signals. Different sizes support different speeds.

Transceiver Form Factors (Sizes)

Transceivers come in different physical sizes. The size determines how fast it goes.

🔔
"Q" means Quad. QSFP has 4 lanes instead of 1. A QSFP28 runs 4 lanes at 25G each = 100G total. A QSFP-DD is "Double Density" - 8 lanes. Understanding the lane math helps you recommend the right cable (how many fibers the cable needs matches the transceiver's lane count).

OEM vs. Third-Party ("Alternative") Transceivers

This is where we make serious margin and deliver serious value.

Switch manufacturers (Cisco, Arista, Juniper, etc.) sell their own branded transceivers. These are called OEM optics. They work, but they cost 3-10x more than third-party alternatives that do the same job.

The LAN Cables Direct Advantage

We sell third-party "alternative" transceivers that are coded for the customer's switch platform. Same specs, same performance, same standards (IEEE, MSA) - at a fraction of the OEM price.

"Coded for" means our transceivers identify themselves as compatible with the specific switch brand. Cisco, Arista, Juniper, Dell, Mellanox/NVIDIA - we cover them all.

Our optics carry a 5-year warranty and ship with DOM (Digital Optical Monitoring) support so the customer can monitor signal health in real time.

Technical Detail

MSA compliance: MSA (Multi-Source Agreement) is the industry standard that defines physical dimensions and electrical interfaces for transceivers. Any MSA-compliant transceiver will physically fit and electronically work in any MSA-compliant switch port. OEM vendors add software locks to reject third-party modules. Our coding addresses this. The optic itself is identical in design.

DOM / DDM: Digital Optical Monitoring reports TX power, RX power, temperature, voltage, and bias current. Our transceivers fully support DOM so the customer's monitoring tools work exactly as expected.

AOCs & DACs - The Short-Range Champions

▾
The Simple Version

Sometimes two devices are right next to each other - in the same rack or one rack over. Using a separate transceiver + cable for a short connection is like hiring a moving truck to carry a pizza across the street.

DACs and AOCs are all-in-one cables. The "transceiver" is built right into the cable ends. No separate parts. Plug one end into one switch, the other end into another switch. Done.

DAC vs. AOC - What's the Difference?

⚡
DAC (Direct Attach Copper) / Twinax
Copper cable with transceivers built into each end. Passive (no electronics, up to ~5m) or Active (powered, up to ~10m). Lowest latency, lowest cost, lowest power.
💡
AOC (Active Optical Cable)
Fiber cable with transceivers built into each end. Always active (powered). Reaches 10-100m. Thinner, lighter, better airflow than DAC at longer lengths.

Sales Tip

Ask about distance first. If the run is under 5 meters, a passive DAC is the most cost-effective choice. 5-10 meters, consider active DAC or AOC. Over 10 meters, AOC or separate transceiver + fiber is the path. These are coded to platform just like our standalone transceivers.

DACs and AOCs are available in all major form factors: SFP+ (10G), SFP28 (25G), QSFP+ (40G), QSFP28 (100G), QSFP56 (200G), QSFP-DD (400G).

IEC Power Cords

▾
The Simple Version

Every computer in the data center needs electricity. IEC power cords are the cables that connect servers and switches to the power strip in the rack (the PDU).

They come in different plug shapes depending on how much power the device needs. Think of it like how a regular lamp uses a normal plug, but an oven uses a bigger, different-shaped plug.

Common IEC Connector Types

Connector PairAmpsUsed For
C13/C1410A-15AStandard servers, switches, most equipment
C19/C2016A-20AHigh-power servers, blade chassis, storage arrays
C15/C1610A-15AHigher temperature-rated devices (some Cisco)

C13/C14 is the most common. C19/C20 is for power-hungry equipment. These are consumables - data centers buy them in volume.

Sales Context

Power cords are a low-margin, high-volume, easy add-on. Any time you're quoting cables or optics, ask: "Do you need power cords for this deployment?" It's usually a yes, and it increases order value with zero additional selling effort. We carry standard North American (NEMA) plugs. Color options available for cable management (red for A-feed, blue for B-feed is common).

What Our Cables Plug Into - Patch Panels

▾
The Simple Version

Imagine you have 48 garden hoses going to different parts of your yard. Instead of having them all hang loose, you mount a board on the wall with 48 labeled holes. Each hose connects to its labeled hole. Now when you need to switch which hose goes where, you only change the short connecting piece on the front - you never touch the long hose running through the wall.

That's a patch panel. It's an organized wall of connection ports mounted in the rack. Permanent cables land on the back. Short "patch" cables on the front connect to the switch. Clean, organized, easy to change.

We don't sell patch panels, but your customers use them with our cables. Understanding how they work makes you a better consultant.

The patch panel sits between the switch and the permanent cabling. Our LC patch cables go on the front. Our MTP trunks go on the back. The customer swaps the front cables, not the permanent ones.

Types of Patch Panels

▶ Copper Patch Panels (RJ45)

A flat 1U or 2U panel with 24 or 48 RJ45 ports on the front. Permanent copper cables (from wall jacks or other racks) punch down on the back. Short copper patch cables (our CAT6A) connect from the front to the switch.

Think of a telephone switchboard - the old-school kind with plugs. Same concept, modern execution.

▶ Fiber Patch Panels (LC Adapter Panels)

Same concept as copper, but for fiber. LC duplex adapters mounted on a panel. Trunk cables land on the back, LC patch cables on the front. Common in high-density fiber deployments where cassettes aren't used.

These come in various densities: 24-port, 48-port, 96-port LC. They mount in standard rack enclosures.

Fiber Cassettes - The MTP-to-LC Bridge

▾
The Simple Version

Remember the MTP connector that carries 12 fibers at once? The switch has individual LC ports. Something needs to split the bundle into individual connections.

A fiber cassette is a small plastic box that does exactly this. The MTP trunk cable plugs into the back of the cassette. The front has individual LC ports. Inside the box, the 12 fibers are permanently fanned out and connected.

Think of it like a power strip adapter - one big plug on one side, many small outlets on the other.

A fiber cassette converts one MTP-12 connection into 6 LC duplex connections. Our MTP trunks plug into the back. Our LC patch cables plug into the front. The cassette sits in between.
🔔
Why you need to know this: When a customer orders MTP trunk cables, they're running those trunks to cassettes or breakout panels. Understanding the cassette means you can confirm the right polarity and fiber count. If they order an MTP-12 trunk, they'll use it with a 12-fiber cassette that fans out to 6 LC duplex ports. If they order an MTP-24 trunk, it fans to 12 LC duplex ports. The math has to match.

Qualifying a Lead - The Questions That Matter

▾

Judge competence by how complete a spec is. Vagueness means they don't know or don't care. Both are red flags you'll need to address.

The Five Questions

1
What are you connecting?
Server to switch? Switch to switch? Building to building? This tells you the product category (copper, fiber, DAC, AOC) and the distance.
2
What speed?
1G, 10G, 25G, 40G, 100G, 400G? This determines the cable grade, transceiver form factor, and fiber count.
3
What distance?
Under 5m? DAC territory. 5-100m? AOC or fiber with transceivers. Over 100m? Singlemode fiber. Distance is the first filter.
4
What equipment (switch/server brand and model)?
Cisco Nexus? Arista 7050? Dell S-series? Juniper QFX? Mellanox/NVIDIA ConnectX? This tells you what coding the transceivers need and confirms port compatibility.
5
How many?
One rack or a full deployment? Quantity shapes pricing, lead time, and whether to offer project-level support.
Pro Tip

If they can't answer these five questions, help them figure it out. That's consultative selling. Walk them through it. The customer who needed help specifying becomes the customer who trusts you for the next 20 orders. Concede what you don't know ("I want to make sure I'm recommending the right thing - let me verify the compatibility on that switch model") and get back to them fast.
