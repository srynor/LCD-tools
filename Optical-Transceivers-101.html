Foundations

What is an optical transceiver?

A transceiver is the little brick that plugs into a switch or server port. It takes electrical signals from the host and turns them into pulses of light to send down a fiber. On the other end, another transceiver does the reverse. Transmit + receive in one module - that is where "transceiver" comes from.

Anatomy of a transceiver
Every transceiver contains the same building blocks. The EEPROM (gold) is the only thing that makes one "Cisco" and another "Arista" - the silicon is often identical.

The two jobs

TX (transmit): A driver chip pulses a laser on and off thousands of millions of times per second. The laser is either a VCSEL (multimode, cheap, short reach) or a DFB / EML (single-mode, longer reach).

RX (receive): A photodiode catches light coming back the other way, a transimpedance amplifier (TIA) boosts the tiny current, and the bits are recovered.

Bits, baud, and encoding

NRZ - Non-Return-to-Zero. One bit per symbol. Used up through 25G per lane.

PAM4 - Pulse Amplitude Modulation, 4 levels. Two bits per symbol. The only way to push 50G+ per lane without doubling the baud rate. Used from 50G upward.

Coherent - Modulates phase + amplitude + polarization. Requires a DSP in the module. The only way to do ZR / ZR+ at 80km+. Power-hungry and expensive.

Vocabulary check

A "lane" is one transmit + receive pair. A 100G QSFP28 LR4 module has 4 optical lanes running 25G each, all sharing one duplex fiber pair via different wavelengths. A 400G QSFP-DD DR4 module has 4 electrical input lanes from the switch ASIC, each at 100G PAM4. Lane count, lane speed, and modulation are the three numbers that define the optic.

Form factors - the physical packages

A form factor is the physical size and pin layout of the module. They are standardized by Multi-Source Agreements (MSAs) so any compliant optic fits any compliant port. You will see four families in the data center today.

Light, wavelengths, and the bands

Optical fiber does not carry "data." It carries light. The wavelength of that light - measured in nanometers (nm) - determines what kind of fiber it works on, how far it reaches, and how much it costs.

The optical spectrum used in data centers
Wavelength (λ) and frequency (f) are inversely related: λ × f = c (speed of light). Shorter wavelength = higher frequency. The three regions used in the DC are 850nm for short multimode runs, 1310nm for medium single-mode, and 1550nm for long single-mode and coherent DWDM.

Why these three wavelengths and not others?

850 nm

Why it exists: VCSEL lasers at 850nm are cheap to manufacture - tens of cents in volume. That makes 850nm the only sane choice for short, high-volume server-rack runs.

The catch: only works over multimode fiber (OM3, OM4, OM5). Multimode signal degrades fast at distance because different light rays take slightly different paths inside the fiber. Hard ceiling around 400m even on OM4.

You will hear: SR, SR4, SR8.

1310 nm (O-band)

Why it exists: 1310nm is the "zero-dispersion" window of standard single-mode fiber. Light at this wavelength does not spread out much as it travels - so the receiver still sees clean pulses at 10km.

The catch: attenuation is higher than 1550nm. Past 40km it gets uneconomical without amplifiers.

You will hear: DR, FR, LR, LR4, CWDM4.

1550 nm (C-band)

Why it exists: the absolute minimum-loss window in glass fiber. About 0.2 dB/km of attenuation. That is what makes 80km, 120km, and 400km+ reaches possible.

The catch: chromatic dispersion is significant. You need a DSP and coherent modulation to recover the signal cleanly past 40km. That is where ZR / ZR+ optics come in.

You will hear: ER, ZR, ZR+, DWDM.

WDM grids - many wavelengths on one fiber

Wavelength Division Multiplexing puts multiple wavelengths of light on a single fiber pair, each carrying its own data stream. Like running 4 lanes of traffic down one highway, color-coded so no one swaps lanes.

CWDM Coarse

Spacing: 20 nm between channels

Channels: up to 18, from 1271-1611 nm

Where used: CWDM4 in 100GBASE-CWDM4 and 400GBASE-FR4. Channels 1271 / 1291 / 1311 / 1331.

Cheap, simple, no temperature control needed on the lasers.

LWDM LAN WDM

Spacing: ~800 GHz (~4.5 nm) around 1310

Channels: 4, tightly packed around the O-band zero-dispersion point

Where used: 100GBASE-LR4, 400GBASE-LR4. Channels 1295.56 / 1300.05 / 1304.58 / 1309.14.

Better dispersion performance than CWDM at 10km+.

DWDM Dense

Spacing: 100 GHz or 50 GHz (~0.8 nm or ~0.4 nm)

Channels: 80+ in C-band alone

Where used: coherent ZR / ZR+ transceivers for DCI. Temperature-controlled lasers required.

The whole field of long-haul fiber optics rides on the DWDM grid.

Frequency vs wavelength - quick math

If a customer or vendor sees a frequency in GHz or THz instead of a wavelength, they are talking DWDM. Convert with f (THz) = 299792.458 / λ (nm). So 1550 nm = 193.4 THz. ITU C-band channel 34 is 193.4 THz. They are the same thing said two ways.

The "4" and "8" after the code

A trailing digit is the lane count. SR4 = 4 parallel optical lanes. SR8 = 8 parallel optical lanes. LR4 = 4 wavelengths multiplexed on one duplex fiber pair. The number tells you how many fibers (parallel) or how many colors (WDM) the optic uses.

Where transceivers live in a data center

Modern data centers are built on a "spine-leaf" or "Clos" fabric. Every server has multiple paths to every other server. The transceivers are not random - each tier of the fabric has its own typical speed and reach.

Spine-leaf fabric - where each optic goes
Server NICs are typically 25G or 100G today. Leaf-to-spine is 100G or 400G. Spine-to-spine or DCI is 400G/800G coherent. AI clusters skip ahead - 800G/1.6T OSFP-RHS for GPU-to-GPU fabric.

Reading a customer's environment

Server-to-ToR (in-rack)

Typical: 25G or 100G

Optics they buy: DAC (cheapest), AOC (slightly longer), SR transceiver + fiber (most flexible)

How many: 24-48 per rack, hundreds of racks. Volume play.

Leaf-to-spine (in-building)

Typical: 100G or 400G

Optics they buy: SR4 / SR8 on MMF inside the room, DR4 / FR4 across rooms

How many: 4-32 uplinks per leaf, dozens of leaves.

DCI - data center to data center

Typical: 100G to 800G coherent

Optics they buy: ZR / ZR+ in QSFP-DD or OSFP. Premium product.

How many: small count, very high margin per module.

AI / HPC cluster

Typical: 400G / 800G / 1.6T

Optics they buy: OSFP-RHS for GPU servers. DAC where reach allows. SR8 / DR8 fabric.

How many: 8+ ports per GPU server, thousands of servers. This is where the hyperscale spend is.

Common confusion to clear up

25G uses the same physical SFP28 footprint as 10G SFP+. A SFP28 port accepts both SFP28 (25G) and SFP+ (10G) modules. But a SFP+ port only accepts SFP+ - it cannot run 25G. If a customer asks "will this 25G module work in my old 10G switch," the answer is usually no, but ask which switch.

Selling angle

QSFP-DD is backward-compatible with QSFP28 ports (a QSFP28 module physically fits a QSFP-DD cage). OSFP is a larger envelope with integrated heatsink and slightly more thermal headroom. Cisco/Arista mostly went QSFP-DD. NVIDIA went OSFP for AI fabric. Both ship in volume. Always ask what cage the customer's switch has.

Customers replacing dead 400G modules under deadline pressure pay attention to lead time - business days vs the OEM's weeks - more than they pay attention to price.

Thermals are the story

An 800G module dissipates 15-18W in a roughly thumb-sized package. That is why OSFP / OSFP-RHS (Riding Heat Sink) is preferred over QSFP-DD800 in dense AI applications - the larger envelope and integrated heatsink dump heat better. The customer's switch design dictates which form factor they need.

The Sale

OEMs don't make their optics

This is the single most important fact in your toolkit. Cisco does not make Cisco optics. Arista does not make Arista optics. Juniper does not make Juniper optics. NVIDIA, the largest optical buyer in the world, openly sources from the same ODMs as everyone else.

The supply chain - same fab, different label
The OEM "manufacturing" step is one chip - an EEPROM coded with the vendor's branding string. Everything else is from the ODM. ODMs publish customer lists in their annual reports.

The EEPROM - that is the whole trick

When you plug an optic into a switch, the switch reads a small EEPROM chip on the module. That EEPROM contains a vendor name, part number, serial number, wavelength, distance code, and calibration constants. The switch compares the vendor name against a "supported transceiver" table and decides whether to enable the port.

That is the entire mechanism. The optical hardware, the laser, the photodiode, the DSP - all identical. The only thing that makes one module a "Cisco" optic and another module the exact same hardware from the exact same factory a "3rd-party" optic is what string the EEPROM returns.

Common myth

"OEM optics are higher quality and better tested." The same ODM line that builds Cisco-coded modules builds the modules we sell. Same wafer lot, same burn-in, same QC. The difference is the sticker and the markup. A senior network engineer at any hyperscaler already knows this. The myth lives at companies that have not done the homework.

What the OEMs themselves admit

Cisco

IOS XE includes the service unsupported-transceiver command. Issuing it lets a Cisco switch accept any MSA-compliant optic. Cisco's published support policy does not void TAC coverage on the switch for unrelated issues. Cisco maintains the command in current releases.

Arista

Arista has been openly accepting of 3rd-party optics from launch. Arista EOS does not require Arista-coded modules to enable a port. Arista TAC will troubleshoot links with 3rd-party optics in use.

NVIDIA

NVIDIA's own networking products use Innolight and other ODMs as primary suppliers. Cumulus / NVIDIA Spectrum switches accept MSA-compliant modules. The InfiniBand cable / transceiver ecosystem is multi-vendor by design.

The Sale

The legal backbone - Magnuson-Moss + Sherman

Two federal laws sit behind every 3rd-party optic conversation. Know what they say. When a customer pushes back with "won't this void my switch warranty," you answer with citation, not opinion.

Federal Statute

Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act

15 U.S.C. §§ 2301-2312 - enacted 1975 - enforced by the FTC.

The act's tie-in sales prohibition (Section 102(c), codified at 15 U.S.C. § 2302(c)) states that a warrantor cannot condition warranty coverage on the consumer's use of branded parts or services unless:

  • The branded parts or services are provided to the consumer without charge, OR
  • The warrantor has applied for and received a waiver from the FTC.

FTC waivers are rare and require the warrantor to prove that no other part will work properly and the warranty would otherwise fail. Cisco, Arista, Juniper, HPE - none have such a waiver on optical transceivers.

What this means in practice

A switch vendor cannot legally void your switch warranty solely because you used a third-party optic. To deny a warranty claim, the vendor must affirmatively prove that the third-party optic was the cause of the failure. That is a high bar - and it does not apply at all to failures unrelated to the optic (a power supply, a fan, a fabric ASIC, a software bug).

The analogy customers understand

A car manufacturer cannot void your engine warranty because you used a non-OEM oil filter. They have to prove the aftermarket filter caused the engine damage. Same principle.

Customer-facing line

"Using our optic does not void your Cisco / Arista / Juniper warranty. Federal law - Magnuson-Moss - prohibits the OEM from conditioning warranty on their branded parts. They would have to prove our optic caused the failure to deny a claim, and that is a high bar."

Citation: 15 U.S.C. §§ 2301-2312. FTC enforcement reference at ftc.gov - search "Magnuson-Moss tie-in sales provisions." See also FTC Staff Compliance Guide on Magnuson-Moss.
Federal Statute

Sherman Antitrust Act

15 U.S.C. §§ 1-7 - enacted 1890 - enforced by the DOJ Antitrust Division and FTC.

Section 1 prohibits contracts and combinations in restraint of trade. Section 2 prohibits monopolization or attempts to monopolize a market.

One pattern Sherman polices directly is the tying arrangement - when a seller of a dominant product (the "tying" product) forces buyers to also purchase a separate product (the "tied" product) as a condition of the sale. When the tying reduces competition in the tied market, it can violate Section 1 or Section 2.

Why this matters in optics

If an OEM made it a requirement that you buy only their-branded optics in order to use your switch under warranty, that would be a textbook tying arrangement in a market (optical transceivers) where independent competitors exist and meet identical MSA specifications. Such a requirement could expose the OEM to Sherman liability - which is one reason OEMs avoid the explicit requirement and rely instead on softer "warning" language and proprietary EEPROM coding.

What it means for the customer

The customer's choice of optical vendor is a protected commercial choice. An OEM cannot legally use its switch monopoly position to lock out the MSA-compliant optical market.

Customer-facing line

"The Sherman Antitrust Act prevents an OEM from tying the use of their switch to the purchase of their optics. You are protected in your choice of optical vendor. The MSA standards exist specifically so that any compliant module works in any compliant port - that is the whole point of the standard."

Citation: 15 U.S.C. §§ 1-7. DOJ Antitrust Division reference at justice.gov/atr. See Eastman Kodak Co. v. Image Technical Services, 504 U.S. 451 (1992) for tying doctrine.

Putting the two together on the call

When a customer hesitates on a 3rd-party optic, the conversation usually goes one of three ways:

"Won't this void my switch warranty?" - Magnuson-Moss says no. The OEM has to prove the optic caused the failure. They cannot deny coverage just because the optic is third-party.

"Cisco / Arista told me I have to buy their optics." - They can prefer their optics commercially. They cannot make it a warranty condition under federal law. Their own command-line interface lets you accept third-party optics. Their TAC will troubleshoot the link.

"What if it fails?" - Our 5-year warranty on the optic itself. We replace, no questions. Business days, not weeks.

Reference

Glossary - the terms that show up in every spec

If you remember nothing else, remember these. Every customer call uses three or four of them.

AOC
Active Optical Cable. Transceiver + fiber bonded as one assembly. Fixed length, no field connectors.
ASIC
Application Specific Integrated Circuit. The custom chip at the heart of a switch (Broadcom Tomahawk, NVIDIA Spectrum, etc.).
BiDi
Bidirectional. Optics that transmit and receive on two different wavelengths over a single fiber strand.
CDR
Clock and Data Recovery. The chip that re-times noisy signals back to clean bits.
Coherent
Modulation scheme that uses phase, amplitude, and polarization. Required for 80km+ reach. Uses a DSP.
CWDM
Coarse Wavelength Division Multiplexing. 20nm channel spacing. Used in CWDM4 (100G, 400G FR4).
DAC
Direct Attach Copper. Bonded twinax cable in a transceiver shell. Cheap, in-rack only.
DCI
Data Center Interconnect. Links between data centers - same campus, metro, or regional.
DFB
Distributed Feedback laser. Narrow linewidth single-mode laser. Used at 1310nm and 1550nm.
DSP
Digital Signal Processor. Required in PAM4 modules at high speeds and in all coherent modules.
DWDM
Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing. 100GHz or 50GHz spacing. C-band, 80+ channels.
EEPROM
Electrically Erasable Programmable Read-Only Memory. The chip in every optic that stores its identity string.
FEC
Forward Error Correction. Math added to the signal to recover bits after errors. Mandatory at PAM4 and coherent.
LWDM
LAN WDM. 800GHz channel spacing around 1310nm. Used in LR4 variants.
MMF
Multimode Fiber. Larger core, supports VCSEL lasers, short reach only. OM3, OM4, OM5.
MPO / MTP
Multi-fiber Push-On connector. MTP is US Conec's high-precision MPO. 12, 16, or 24 fibers per connector.
MSA
Multi-Source Agreement. Industry spec defining the mechanical/electrical interface so any compliant module fits any compliant port.
NRZ
Non-Return-to-Zero. 1 bit per symbol encoding. Used up through 25G per lane.
ODM
Original Design Manufacturer. The contract manufacturer that actually builds an optic - Innolight, Eoptolink, Coherent, etc.
OEM
Original Equipment Manufacturer. The switch / server vendor - Cisco, Arista, Juniper, NVIDIA, HPE, Dell.
OIF
Optical Internetworking Forum. Standards body for high-speed electrical and coherent optical interfaces.
OSFP
Octal Small Form-Factor Pluggable. 8-lane form factor with integrated heatsink. 400G / 800G / 1.6T.
PAM4
Pulse Amplitude Modulation, 4 levels. 2 bits per symbol. Used from 50G per lane upward.
QSFP
Quad Small Form-Factor Pluggable. 4-lane module. Family includes QSFP+, QSFP28, QSFP56, QSFP-DD.
SFF
Small Form Factor Committee. Now under SNIA. Publishes the mechanical MSAs for SFP, QSFP, OSFP, etc.
SFP
Small Form-Factor Pluggable. Single-lane module. Family: SFP, SFP+, SFP28, SFP56.
SMF
Single-Mode Fiber. Smaller core, only one propagation mode. Used for all reach 500m+.
TIA
Transimpedance Amplifier. Converts the tiny photodiode current into a usable voltage signal.
ToR
Top of Rack. The leaf switch at the top of a server rack. Aggregates the servers in that rack.
VCSEL
Vertical-Cavity Surface-Emitting Laser. Low-cost 850nm laser used in all SR-grade MMF optics.
WDM
Wavelength Division Multiplexing. Multiple colors of light on one fiber. CWDM (coarse) or DWDM (dense).
ZR / ZR+
Coherent DCI optics. 80km / 120km+ reach in a pluggable form factor. OIF + OpenZR+ standards.
