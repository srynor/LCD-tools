1 The Basics

What is an optical transceiver?

A transceiver is a small module that plugs into a switch or a server. It turns electrical signals into light, sends that light down a glass fiber, and turns light coming back into electrical signals.

💬
The translator analogy. A switch thinks in electricity. A fiber cable carries light. The transceiver is the translator that sits between them. It speaks electricity on one side and light on the other. The word itself is a mash-up of transmit and receive, because every module does both jobs at once.

The name breaks down simply. Transmitter plus receiver equals transceiver. One side has a tiny laser that flashes light on and off billions of times per second. The other side has a light detector that reads those flashes back into data. Both live in the same module, so a single fiber link can run in both directions.

People also call these optics, modules, pluggables, or by their shape, like SFP or QSFP. They all do the same core job. They convert between electrical and optical and they plug into a slot called a cage or port.

The three things every transceiver decides

1. Speed

How much data per second. From 1 gigabit (1G) to 1.6 terabit (1.6T). Higher numbers move more traffic.

2. Reach

How far the light can travel and still be read. From a few meters in a rack to 80 km or more across a city.

3. Media

What it plugs into. The fiber type and connector on the front, the speed and host on the back.

Get those three right and the link comes up. Get one wrong and it does not. Most of this booklet is really about reading those three things off a label and matching them to your network.

Anatomy of a module

Here is what is actually inside. This is drawn as an SFP, the most common shape, but every transceiver has these same building blocks.

Fig 1Inside an SFP. Light leaves and enters on the left through the LC port. Data enters and leaves on the right through the gold contacts. The TOSA holds the laser, the ROSA holds the detector, and the small EEPROM chip stores the module's identity. More on each of these as we go.

🔑 The one idea to keep

A transceiver is a converter with an ID card. It converts electricity to light and back, and it carries a little memory chip that tells the switch what it is. Everything else is detail on top of those two facts.

2 The Idea

How light carries your data

Computers speak in ones and zeros. A transceiver sends a one as a flash of light and a zero as darkness. Do that billions of times a second down a glass fiber and you have a network link.

Think of an incredibly fast flashlight pointed down a glass tube. Light on means one. Light off means zero. The laser can switch on and off billions of times each second, so a huge amount of data flows in a moment. At the far end, a light detector watches for the flashes and rebuilds the ones and zeros.

Glass fiber is used instead of copper for two reasons. Light travels far with very little loss, and it is immune to electrical noise. That is why a fiber link can run hundreds of meters or many kilometers where copper would give up after a few meters at high speed.

The full journey, end to end

Fig 2One link, four conversions. Electricity becomes light in the sending module, travels the fiber as light, then becomes electricity again in the receiving module. The orange wires are the electrical side. The cyan strand is the optical side.

NRZ and PAM4, or why a flash can mean more than one bit

Early optics used the simple rule, light on equals one and light off equals zero. That scheme is called NRZ. It works well up to about 25 to 28 gigabits per lane.

To go faster without flashing faster, engineers added more brightness levels. PAM4 uses four light levels instead of two. Each flash now carries two bits at once, not one. That doubles the data at the same flash rate. PAM4 is how the industry got to 50G, 100G, and 200G on a single lane. The trade-off is that the four levels sit closer together, so PAM4 is more sensitive to noise and always leans on error correction. More on that later.

Fig 3Two levels versus four. NRZ sends one bit at a time. PAM4 packs two bits into every flash by using four brightness steps. Same flash rate, double the data, at the cost of less margin against noise.

A lane is one independent stream of bits. A simple SFP has one lane. A QSFP has four lanes. The Q in QSFP stands for Quad, meaning four.

Total speed equals lane speed times lane count. Four lanes of 25G gives 100G. Eight lanes of 100G gives 800G. This is the single most useful piece of mental math in optics. When you see 400G as 8x50G, that means eight lanes each running 50 gigabits. When you see it as 4x100G, that is four lanes at 100 gigabits. Same total, fewer lanes, newer technology.

Lanes can be carried as separate colors of light on one fiber, or as separate fibers in a ribbon. That choice is what separates the SR, DR, FR and LR variants you will meet in the wavelengths and speeds sections.

3 Under the Hood

What is inside the module

Four parts do almost all the work. A laser, a detector, a chip that drives them, and a memory chip that holds the module's ID. Knowing these makes every datasheet easier to read.

🔥 TOSA - the transmit side

TOSA stands for Transmitter Optical Sub-Assembly. It holds the laser and the lens that focuses light into the fiber. When the switch sends a one, the driver flashes the laser. The TOSA is where electricity becomes light.

👁 ROSA - the receive side

ROSA stands for Receiver Optical Sub-Assembly. It holds a light detector called a photodiode and an amplifier. It watches the incoming flashes and turns them back into a clean electrical signal. The ROSA is where light becomes electricity.

🧮 The driver, DSP or gearbox

A controller chip shapes the signal. At higher speeds this is a DSP that cleans up PAM4 and can convert lane counts, for example turning eight slow lanes from the switch into four fast lanes of light.

💾 The EEPROM - the ID card

A tiny memory chip stores who made the module, what speed it runs, what wavelength it uses, and how far it reaches. The switch reads this the instant you plug in. The whole next section is about this chip.

Lasers, and why some optics cost more than others

The laser is the single biggest driver of cost and reach. A cheap laser is fine for a short hop inside a rack. A long run across a campus needs a more precise and more expensive laser. Here are the four families you will meet.

The flashlight versus the lighthouse. A VCSEL is a cheap, wide flashlight. Great across a room, useless across a bay. A coherent laser is a tight, tuned lighthouse beam built to be seen 100 km away. You pay for the precision, and you only need it when the distance demands it. This is why matching reach to the job saves real money. There is no point buying a lighthouse to light a closet.

Most optics in a data center use intensity modulation, direct detection, often shortened to IM-DD. The laser changes brightness and the detector simply measures brightness. Simple, cheap, and great for short and medium reach.

For very long distances the industry uses coherent optics. These encode data in the phase and amplitude of the light, not just brightness, and use a powerful DSP to recover it. This is the technology behind 400G-ZR and 400G-ZR+, which can carry 400G across a metro area on a single wavelength. Coherent used to fill a whole line card. It now fits inside a QSFP-DD or OSFP pluggable, which is one of the bigger recent shifts in the industry.

4 The ID Card

The EEPROM, coding, and live diagnostics

Every transceiver carries a small memory chip called the EEPROM. It is the module's ID card and its health monitor. Understanding it explains why one optic works in a switch and an identical-looking one gets rejected.

EEPROM is just a small chunk of memory that keeps its contents when the power is off. The instant you seat a module, the switch reads this chip over a simple two-wire connection. In under a second the switch knows the brand, the model, the speed, the wavelength, the reach, and the fiber type. No drivers, no install. The module introduces itself.

What the chip actually stores

The memory is split into two areas. A static area that never changes, and a live area that updates constantly. Click any field below to see what it holds and why it matters.

🔗 Static info (the ID)

Set at the factory. Brand, part number, serial, supported speeds, wavelength, reach, connector and fiber type.

📉 Live diagnostics, called DOM or DDM

Updated in real time while the link runs. This is your early-warning system for a failing optic or a dirty fiber.

☝ Tap a field above. Each one is a real value the switch can read off the module while it runs.

Wavelength lives in the EEPROM

One of those fields is the wavelength, the color of light the module uses. It is stored right there in the ID area, often as a number like 850, 1310, or 1550 nanometers. This matters because the two ends of a link must agree on color. A module sending 1310 nm light cannot be read by a receiver tuned for 850 nm. When you read an optic's EEPROM, the wavelength field tells you instantly whether it is a short-reach multimode optic or a long-reach single-mode one. The next section is all about why these colors are what they are.

The management standards behind the chip

The layout of the EEPROM is not random. Three published specifications define where each piece of information lives, so any switch can read any vendor's module. You do not need to memorize these, but you will see the names.

SFF-8472

The map for SFP, SFP+ and SFP28. Defines the ID area and the live diagnostics area.

SFF-8636

The map for QSFP, QSFP+ and QSFP28. A paged layout to hold more lanes of data.

CMIS

Common Management Interface Specification. The modern map for QSFP-DD, OSFP and other 200G-per-lane and faster modules.

What "coding" means, and why it matters to you

Here is the part that trips up newcomers. The EEPROM includes the vendor name and part number. Many switch makers check that field and will only fully trust a module whose ID matches their own approved list. Plug in an unrecognized optic and the switch may flag it, throttle it, or refuse to bring the port up.

A third-party or compatible optic is a quality module whose EEPROM has been programmed, or coded, to present the exact ID the target switch expects. The glass, the laser, and the detector are the same class of parts. The difference is the ID written to the chip. A Cisco-coded module reports itself as Cisco. An Arista-coded module reports itself as Arista. This is completely standard practice and it is how the compatible-optics market exists.

🎯 Why this is the heart of the business

Coding is the difference between an optic that lights up on the first try and one that throws an error. It is also where a single wrong digit on an order turns into a return. Coded to the right switch, confirmed before it ships. That is the whole value of buying from someone who checks.

💡 A practical habit

When a new link will not come up, read the DOM before you swap anything. Low or zero RX power usually means a fiber, connector, or polarity problem, not a bad module. A clean TX power with no RX often means the light is leaving fine but not arriving. The chip tells you where to look.

5 The Colors of Light

Wavelengths, and putting many colors on one fiber

Wavelength is just the color of the light. Optics use three main colors, plus clever tricks to stack many colors on a single strand. The color decides how far the light travels and what fiber it needs.

Light has a color, measured in nanometers, written nm. Optics use three main wavelengths. All three are infrared, which means your eye cannot see them. Never assume a fiber is dark. Always treat it as live.

Fig 4Why these three colors. Glass is clearest at certain wavelengths, so the light loses less energy and travels farther. 850 nm is used for cheap short links over multimode. 1310 and 1550 nm sit in the clearest windows and are used for long single-mode runs.

WDM, or many colors down one fiber

Here is the clever part. Different colors of light do not interfere with each other. So you can send several colors down the same fiber at the same time and split them apart at the far end. This is called WDM. It is how one pair of fibers carries four, eight, or even eighty separate data streams.

Fig 5Four streams, one fiber. A combiner (MUX) merges the colors at one end. A splitter (DEMUX) separates them at the other. This is exactly how a 100GBASE-LR4 optic puts four 25G streams of different colors onto a single pair of fibers.

CWDM - coarse

Colors spaced far apart, about 20 nm. The lasers do not need cooling, so it is cheaper. Used inside many 40G and 100G optics like CWDM4. Good for short and medium reach.

DWDM - dense

Colors packed very close, under 1 nm apart, on a precise grid. Lasers are cooled and tuned. Fits dozens of channels on one fiber for long haul and connecting data centers across a city.

6 The Cable Itself

Fiber types and connectors

A transceiver is only half a link. The fiber and the connector are the other half. Two fiber families and two connector styles cover almost everything you will see.

Multimode versus single-mode

The difference is the size of the glass core in the middle of the fiber, where the light actually travels.

Fig 6The core is everything. Multimode has a wide core, so light takes many paths and slowly smears. That caps the distance. Single-mode has a tiny core, so light travels one clean path and goes far. The optics match the fiber, so an SR module needs multimode and an LR module needs single-mode.

💡 Fast rule of thumb

Aqua or lime jacket usually means multimode and short reach. Yellow jacket usually means single-mode and long reach. It is a rule, not a law, so confirm against the optic, but it is right far more often than not.

Connectors - LC and MPO

Two connector styles cover most of the data center. The choice depends on whether the optic uses two fibers or many.

Fig 7Two fibers or many. An LC carries one fiber to send and one to receive. It suits optics that use a single beam each way, like LR4 and FR4. An MPO (the branded version is MTP from US CONEC) packs 8, 12, 16 or 24 fibers into one connector, which suits parallel optics like SR4, DR4 and SR8.

Simplex

One fiber, used with special optics that send and receive on the same strand using two colors.

Duplex

Two fibers, the everyday setup. One to transmit, one to receive. This is the LC world.

Parallel

Many fibers at once, each a separate lane. This is the MPO world, used by SR4, DR4 and the breakouts you will meet soon.

With parallel optics, every transmit fiber on one end must land on the matching receive fiber at the other end. Get that mapping wrong, called polarity, and the link stays dark even though every part is good. MPO systems use defined polarity methods, often labeled A, B and C, to keep this straight. Order the cassettes and trunks as a matched set and the polarity is handled for you.

The other big one is dirt. A single speck of dust on a fiber end face can block enough light to drop a high-speed link. The faster the optic, the less margin it has for contamination. Inspect and clean end faces before mating. More links are saved by a cleaning swab than by a new transceiver.

7 The Shapes

Form factors - the shape of the module

A form factor is the physical shape of the module and the slot it fits into. The shape sets the size, the lane count, and therefore the top speed. Learn the family names and the whole catalog falls into place.

The names look like alphabet soup at first. They are not. There is a simple logic.

SFP = small, 1 lane

Small Form-factor Pluggable. The little single-lane module. SFP, SFP+, SFP28 and SFP56 are the same shape at faster lane speeds.

QSFP = quad, 4 lanes

The Q is Quad, meaning four lanes in one module. Wider than an SFP. QSFP+, QSFP28 and QSFP56 step up the lane speed.

DD = double density, 8 lanes

QSFP-DD adds a second row of electrical contacts to fit eight lanes in a QSFP-sized body. That is how it reaches 400G and 800G.

OSFP = octal, 8 lanes

Octal means eight. A slightly larger body built from the start for eight high-speed lanes and the heat that comes with them. The path to 800G and 1.6T.

Explore the family

Click any module to see its specs. The colored bar shows its relative footprint.

Side by side, roughly to scale

Fig 8Bigger body, more lanes, more heat. Faster modules need more electrical lanes and a way to shed more heat, so the bodies grow. Notice QSFP-DD keeps the QSFP footprint, which is why it can accept older QSFP optics. OSFP is its own slightly larger shape with a built-in heatsink area.

🔑 The compatibility rule that saves money

A faster port usually accepts the slower module of the same family. A QSFP-DD port takes QSFP28 and QSFP+ optics. A QSFP28 port takes QSFP+ optics. This lets you buy a fast switch now and light it with the optics you already own. OSFP is the exception. OSFP ports take OSFP modules, and reaching a QSFP optic needs a small adapter.

XFP was an early 10G module, larger than SFP+, now mostly retired. CFP, CFP2 and CFP4 were the first 40G and 100G shapes, large and power-hungry, replaced by QSFP28 for most uses but still found on some long-haul and coherent gear. SFP-DD and SFP112 push the small SFP body to 100G on a single lane for high-density server links. QSFP-DD800 is QSFP-DD running eight lanes at 100G each for 800G. You do not need these on day one, but the names will appear on old and new gear alike.

9 Read the Label

Decode any optic name in five seconds

Names like 100GBASE-LR4 look cryptic until you know the pattern. There are only four parts, and once you see them you can read any optic on a datasheet.

The reach letter is the one that matters most

The middle letter tells you how far the optic reaches and what fiber it needs. Memorize these six and you are most of the way there.

S = Short

Multimode, up to about 100m. The cheap in-rack and in-row optic. Example SR, SR4, SR8.

D = 500 meters

Single-mode, 500m, one wavelength per fiber. The data center workhorse. Example DR, DR4, DR8.

F = 2 kilometers

Single-mode, 2km. Building to building on a campus. Example FR, FR4.

L = 10 kilometers

Single-mode, 10km. The classic long reach. Example LR, LR4.

E = 40 kilometers

Single-mode, 40km. Extended reach across a metro. Example ER, ER4.

Z = 80 km, coherent

Single-mode, 80km and beyond, usually coherent. Connecting data centers. Example ZR, ZR+.

Other letters you will see: T means twisted-pair copper, C points to CWDM colors, and B means BiDi, which sends both directions on a single fiber using two colors. The trailing number is the count of lanes or wavelengths, so the 4 in LR4 means four colors and the 8 in SR8 means eight fibers.

10 Where They Live

NIC and switch - the two homes for a transceiver

Every link has a transceiver at both ends. One end is usually a server's network card. The other is a switch. The jobs are the same, but the context is different.

🖥 The NIC side - the server

A NIC is the network card inside a server. It usually has one or two ports. The transceiver plugs into the NIC and a fiber runs from there to the switch. Server speeds have climbed fast, from 10G and 25G to 100G, 200G and now 400G for AI servers. A smarter NIC with its own processor is called a SmartNIC or a DPU.

🏢 The switch side - the network

A switch aggregates many links. The ToR switch sits at the top of each rack and every server in the rack connects to it. ToR switches connect up to leaf and spine switches that tie the whole data center together. Switch ports are dense and fast, which is exactly why breakouts exist.

Fig 10Optics everywhere. Servers connect up to a ToR switch. ToR switches connect to leaf and spine switches above them. Every one of those lines ends in a transceiver on each side. The faster the layer, the more optics, which is why a single switch can hold hundreds of modules.

🔗 A quick word on the in-rack short hops

Not every short link needs an optical transceiver. For a few meters inside the same rack, many teams use a direct attach copper cable or an active optical cable instead, because it is cheaper for that distance. This booklet is about optical transceivers, so the rule of thumb is simple. Very short, in the rack, copper can win. Anything longer, optical transceivers over fiber carry the load.

11 One Into Many

Breakouts - splitting a fast port into several

A breakout turns one high-speed switch port into several lower-speed links. It is one of the most common and most money-saving setups in the data center.

A switch has a limited number of ports, and each one is fast. Servers are often slower than a single switch port. A breakout solves this. It takes one 400G port and splits it into four 100G links, each going to a different server. The lanes were already separate inside the optic, so the split is natural.

Fig 11One 400G becomes four 100G. A 400GBASE-DR4 optic has four single-mode lanes. A breakout cable or harness sends each lane to its own server. You light four servers from one switch port and one optic, which saves ports and money.
40G → 4 × 10G

The original breakout. A QSFP+ feeding four 10G server links.

100G → 4 × 25G

A QSFP28 SR4 split to four 25G servers. Very common in the 25G server era.

400G → 4 × 100G

Today's workhorse. A QSFP-DD or OSFP DR4 feeding four 100G servers.

⚠ The catch to confirm

Breakouts only work when the switch port supports that breakout mode and both ends agree on lanes, speed and error correction. The optic, the cable, and the switch config all have to line up. This is a place where a quick compatibility check before you order prevents a frustrating afternoon at the rack.

12 The In-Rack Story

DAC and AOC - the short-reach alternatives

Not every link needs a transceiver and a separate fiber. For the shortest hops, two all-in-one cables often win on price and power. Here is when each one beats a pluggable optic, and when it does not.

A pluggable transceiver plus a fiber is the flexible choice. For a one or two meter hop inside the same rack, it can also be more than you need. Two fixed cables fill that gap. A DAC and an AOC. Both arrive with the connector ends built on at the factory, so you buy one sealed assembly instead of two optics and a patch cord.

Fig 12Three ways to make a short link. A DAC is copper with the ends molded on. An AOC is fiber with the optics sealed in. A transceiver plus fiber keeps the two parts separate, so you can swap either one. The first two are cheaper for a short hop. The third gives you reach and freedom to re-patch.

🧱 DAC - direct attach copper

A DAC is a copper twinax cable with module housings molded onto both ends. No laser, no glass. The housing seats in a normal SFP or QSFP cage, but inside it is shielded copper.

It is the cheapest link you can buy, with the lowest power draw and the lowest latency.

Two kinds exist. A passive DAC carries the raw signal and reaches about 3 to 5 m. An active DAC, sometimes called an AEC, adds small chips to clean the signal and stretch reach to roughly 7 m.

Best for server to top-of-rack inside the same rack.

🔌 AOC - active optical cable

An AOC is a fiber cable with the optics permanently attached at both ends. Picture two transceivers joined by a fiber and sealed as one part.

It is thinner, lighter, and longer than a DAC. Common reaches run to about 30 m, and some go much farther.

It shrugs off electrical noise and routes easily in bulk between rows.

Best for row to row and longer in-room hops where a DAC runs out of distance.

The connector ends on a DAC and an AOC carry an EEPROM, the same as a transceiver. They report an identity to the switch and they can be coded to a specific host. A DAC coded for one vendor can be rejected by another. So the coded-to-your-switch rule from the EEPROM section applies to these cables, not just to optics.

13 The Rules

What is an MSA, and who makes the rules

An MSA is the reason a module from one company drops straight into another company's switch. Without it, every vendor's optics would be a closed island. Three groups of rule-makers work together to make interoperability happen.

MSA stands for Multi-Source Agreement. It is a voluntary pact among several manufacturers to build a module to one shared specification. They agree on the exact size, the pin layout, the power, and the management, so a buyer can get the same part from multiple sources. That is the whole point. Multi-source means you are not locked to one supplier.

An MSA is not a government or a formal standards body. It is the industry agreeing with itself, because interchangeable parts grow the whole market. The QSFP-DD MSA and the OSFP MSA are two big examples that define today's highest-speed shapes.

Three layers, three rule-makers

It helps to see who defines what. A finished optic obeys three sets of rules at once.

Fig 14Three rule-makers, one module. IEEE defines how the light should carry data. The MSA defines the physical module and slot. SFF and CMIS define the ID chip. A working optic satisfies all three, which is why any compliant module from any vendor interoperates.

💡 The plain-English takeaway

IEEE decides the language the light speaks. The MSA decides the shape of the plug. SFF and CMIS decide how the module introduces itself. Compatible optics follow all three, then code the ID to your switch. That is the entire trick to interchangeable optics.

14 The Ecosystem

Who actually makes a transceiver

No single company makes a whole optic from raw sand to finished module. It is a layered supply chain. Knowing the layers explains why a compatible optic can match an OEM optic in quality.

1. Component makers - the raw ingredients

The glass, the connectors, the lasers, and the chips. Corning is a leading optical fiber and glass maker. US CONEC makes the MTP connectors. Broadcom and Marvell make the DSP and electronics. 15 Making It Work

Compatibility, FEC, and why links fail

Most failed links are not bad modules. They are a mismatch. Four things have to agree for a link to come up clean. Here is what they are and how to check them.

FEC, the safety net for fast signals

FEC stands for Forward Error Correction. The optic sends a little extra math along with the data so the receiver can fix small errors on the fly. At 25G per lane and above, and always with PAM4, FEC is not optional. The four close levels of PAM4 produce errors that FEC quietly cleans up.

The catch is that both ends must run the same FEC setting. If the switch expects RS-FEC and the other end has it off, the link may flap, run slow, or never come up. When a 25G or faster link misbehaves, FEC mismatch is one of the first things to check.

The four things that must agree

Coding

The EEPROM ID must match what the switch expects, or it may reject the optic.

Speed and reach

Both ends and the fiber must support the same speed and the distance involved.

Fiber and polarity

Multimode optic needs multimode fiber. Parallel optics need correct MPO polarity.

FEC

The error correction setting must match on both ends of the link.

A beginner's troubleshooting order

When a new link will not come up, work in this order. It moves from cheapest to most involved, and the EEPROM helps at almost every step.

Check the live diagnostics. Is the far end's light arriving. Zero or very low RX power points at the fiber, the connector, or polarity, not the module. Good TX with no RX means light is leaving but not landing.

Inspect and clean the end faces. A speck of dust drops more links than a bad laser. Reseat both connectors firmly. This fixes a surprising number of faults for the price of a cleaning swab.

Multimode optic on multimode fiber, single-mode on single-mode. For MPO parallel links, verify the polarity method matches across trunks and cassettes. A swapped TX and RX pair leaves the link dark with no error on the parts themselves.

Does the switch see the optic as supported. Does the port log a coding or vendor error. Is FEC set the same on both ends. This is where a part coded to the wrong switch reveals itself, and where a quick call to your supplier saves the day.

Is the run within the optic's rated distance. For breakouts, is the switch port configured for the breakout and does each branch agree on speed and FEC. A 10km optic will not magically reach 40km, and a port not set to break out will only light one branch.

📞 When to stop guessing

If the DOM looks healthy, the fiber is clean, and the coding and FEC match, but the link still will not behave, that is the moment to call your supplier with the part numbers and the port logs in hand. A supplier who coded and tested the optic can confirm the match fast. Bad news goes to the phone, not another hour of swapping parts.

The lane math

total speed = lane speed × lane count

4 × 25G = 100G
8 × 50G = 400G
8 × 100G = 800G
8 × 200G = 1.6T

Connectors

LC two fibers, duplex optics like LR4, FR4
MPO / MTP many fibers, parallel optics like SR4, DR4, SR8

Duplex uses colors on one pair. Parallel uses one fiber per lane.

17 Plain-English Glossary

Every term, defined simply

If an acronym in this booklet ever loses you, it is here.

AOC
Active Optical Cable. A fixed cable with optics built into both ends, for short hops.
BiDi
Bidirectional. Sends both directions on a single fiber using two colors of light.
CMIS
Common Management Interface Specification. The modern EEPROM and control layout for QSFP-DD, OSFP and faster modules.
Coherent
A long-reach optical method that encodes data in the phase and amplitude of light. Powers ZR optics.
Coding
Programming a module's EEPROM so it reports the identity a specific switch expects.
CWDM
Coarse Wavelength Division Multiplexing. Several colors spaced far apart on one fiber. Cheaper, no laser cooling.
DAC
Direct Attach Cable. A copper cable with connectors built in, for very short in-rack links.
DFB
Distributed Feedback laser. A precise single-mode laser used for medium and long reach.
DOM / DDM
Digital Optical Monitoring. Live readings of temperature, voltage, and light power from the module.
DR
A 500m single-mode reach class, one wavelength per fiber.
DSP
Digital Signal Processor. Cleans and reshapes high-speed signals, essential for PAM4 optics.
DWDM
Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing. Many colors packed close on one fiber for long haul.
EEPROM
The small memory chip in a module that stores its identity and supports diagnostics.
EML
Electro-absorption Modulated Laser. A fast single-mode laser for higher speeds and longer reach.
ER
Extended Reach, about 40km on single-mode fiber.
FEC
Forward Error Correction. Extra data that lets the receiver fix errors. Required at 25G per lane and up.
FR
A 2km single-mode reach class.
IEEE
The standards body that defines Ethernet signaling, such as 100GBASE-LR4 and 800G under 802.3df.
IM-DD
Intensity Modulation, Direct Detection. The simple brightness-based method for short and medium reach.
ITU-T
A standards body that defines wavelength grids used in WDM and coherent systems.
LC
A small two-fiber connector, the everyday duplex connector.
LR
Long Reach, about 10km on single-mode fiber.
MMF
Multimode Fiber. Wide core, short reach, cheaper optics. OM3, OM4, OM5.
MPO
Multi-fiber Push-On connector. Packs 8 to 24 fibers in one connector for parallel optics.
MSA
Multi-Source Agreement. A vendor pact on a shared spec so modules interchange.
MTP
A high-performance branded MPO connector from US CONEC.
NIC
Network Interface Card. The network adapter in a server.
NRZ
Non-Return-to-Zero. The simple two-level signal, one bit per flash.
OSFP
Octal Small Form-factor Pluggable. An eight-lane module shape for 400G, 800G and 1.6T.
PAM4
Four-level signaling that carries two bits per flash. Used from 50G per lane upward.
PSM4
Parallel Single-Mode, four lanes, each on its own fiber over an MPO connector.
QSFP
Quad Small Form-factor Pluggable. A four-lane module shape.
ROSA
Receiver Optical Sub-Assembly. Holds the light detector.
RS-FEC
Reed-Solomon Forward Error Correction. The common FEC scheme at high speeds.
SerDes
Serializer / Deserializer. The switch chip circuit that drives each electrical lane.
SFF
Small Form Factor specifications. Defines EEPROM layouts such as SFF-8472 and SFF-8636.
SFP
Small Form-factor Pluggable. The common single-lane module shape.
SMF
Single-Mode Fiber. Narrow core, long reach, pricier optics. OS2.
SR
Short Reach over multimode, up to about 100m.
TOSA
Transmitter Optical Sub-Assembly. Holds the laser.
ToR
Top of Rack. The switch at the top of a rack that all its servers connect to.
VCSEL
A low-cost 850nm laser used for short multimode links.
WDM
Wavelength Division Multiplexing. Many colors of light sharing one fiber.
ZR
An 80km plus coherent reach class for connecting data centers.
