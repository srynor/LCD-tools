The Analogy

Think of a hotel.
The hotel has floors. Each floor has rooms.
Every floor has one elevator bank - that's the leaf, a top-of-rack switch.
The elevator shafts run straight up through every floor to the roof. Those shafts are the spines - the core switches at the top.

The Rule

A guest on Floor 2 wants to visit a guest on Floor 7.
They don't walk hallways between floors.
They go up to the roof, then back down. Two moves. Every time.
That's 2 hops - leaf up to spine, spine back down to leaf. It never changes, no matter how many floors you add.

Why It Matters

Older networks (called three-tier - core, distribution, access) were like a hotel where some floors had secret shortcuts to other floors. Fast if you knew the shortcuts. A disaster to troubleshoot when one broke.

Leaf-spine removes all the shortcuts. One path. Predictable. If something breaks, you know exactly where to look.

Scaling It

Need more servers? Add a leaf (another floor).
Need more bandwidth between floors? Add a spine (another elevator shaft).
You never touch what's already running.

Where the Fiber Lives

Every leaf connects to every spine with a dedicated uplink - typically 100G or 400G today, sometimes 800G in AI/ML builds.

A 4-leaf / 2-spine fabric has 8 uplink runs minimum.
A 16-leaf / 4-spine fabric has 64.

That's a lot of pre-terminated MTP trunk, and every one of those links needs to come up clean the first time.
